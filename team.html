Team Members

Alexandre Assouline, Principal Investigator

alexandre.assouline@neel.cnrs.fr

Alexandre's research focuses on the electronic properties of solids in which macroscopic quantum phases emerge from the interaction of a large number of particles. These materials, strictly two-dimensional like graphene, exhibit rich and novel physics due to their reduced dimensionality. In particular, they can host exotic quasiparticles such as anyons, which are of great interest for quantum information because of their topological properties, making them less sensitive to decoherence—one of the major challenges in modern physics. After completing his PhD at ESPCI Paris on the anomalous Josephson effect under the supervision of Hervé Aubin, he joined Preden Roulleau’s group at CEA Saclay, and then Andrea Young’s group at the University of California Santa Barbara as a postdoctoral researcher. Recruited by the CNRS in 2023, he was awarded an ERC grant in 2026, dedicated to the study of anyons for topological quantum information.

Mohammad Mulla-Yusuf, PhD student

mohammad.mulla-yusuf@neel.cnrs.fr

Project: Sound-Driven Quasiparticles

Mohammad’s research focuses on the transport of single quantum-coherent electrons using sound waves. By placing ultra-clean bilayer graphene heterostructures on piezoelectric substrates, he transports electrons via surface acoustic waves generated by interdigitated transducers operating at GHz frequencies.

Fatima Bachir, Undergrad

fatima.bachir@neel.cnrs.fr

Project: Anyon Box in Bilayer Graphene

Fatima’s research focuses on developing electrostatic potential to trap single anyons. By realizing quantum capacitance measurements under strong magnetic field, she is able to infer the fractional charge of the trapped anyons.

Nathan Wack, Undergrad

nathan.wack@neel.cnrs.fr

Project: Anyon Box in Bilayer Graphene

Nathan’s research focuses on probing the entropy of anyons confined within electrostatic potential traps. By combining quantum capacitance and noise measurements, he will study the remaining anyon active degree of freedom at ultra low temperature.
