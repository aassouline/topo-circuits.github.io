Team Members

Alexandre Assouline — Principal Investigator

alexandre.assouline@neel.cnrs.fr

Alexandre’s research focuses on the electronic properties of quantum materials in which macroscopic phases emerge from many-body interactions. In two-dimensional systems such as graphene, reduced dimensionality gives rise to rich and novel physics. These systems can host exotic quasiparticles such as anyons, which are of strong interest for quantum information due to their topological properties and reduced sensitivity to decoherence.

After completing his PhD at ESPCI Paris on the anomalous Josephson effect under the supervision of Hervé Aubin, he joined Preden Roulleau’s group at CEA Saclay and then Andrea Young’s group at the University of California, Santa Barbara as a postdoctoral researcher. He was recruited by CNRS in 2023 and awarded an ERC grant in 2026 to develop a research program on anyons for topological quantum information.

Mohammad Mulla-Yusuf — PhD Student

mohammad.mulla-yusuf@neel.cnrs.fr

Project: Sound-Driven Quasiparticles

Mohammad’s research focuses on the transport of single quantum-coherent electrons using surface acoustic waves. By integrating ultra-clean bilayer graphene heterostructures on piezoelectric substrates, he enables controlled electron transport via GHz-frequency acoustic excitations generated by interdigitated transducers.

Fatima Bachir — Undergraduate Student

fatima.bachir@neel.cnrs.fr

Project: Anyon Box in Bilayer Graphene

Fatima’s research focuses on developing electrostatic potentials to trap individual anyons in fractional quantum Hall systems. Using quantum capacitance measurements under strong magnetic fields, she probes the fractional charge of these quasiparticles.

Nathan Wack — Undergraduate Student

nathan.wack@neel.cnrs.fr

Project: Anyon Box in Bilayer Graphene

Nathan’s research focuses on probing the entropy of anyons confined in electrostatic potential traps. By combining quantum capacitance and noise measurements, he investigates their remaining internal degrees of freedom at ultra-low temperatures.
